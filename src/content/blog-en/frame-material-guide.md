## Most People Only Judge a Frame on "Does It Look Good"

When choosing a frame, most people's first glance goes to the shape, color, and brand. Those matter, of course — but judging by looks alone is only seeing half the picture.

You might wear a pair of glasses 10+ hours a day. Material determines everything looks can't tell you: **whether it presses uncomfortably on your nose after hours of wear, whether it triggers an allergic reaction, whether it can be adjusted to fit your face, and how many years it'll hold up.** The same style in a different material can feel completely different on your face.

This guide breaks down the main frame materials on the market, so next time you're choosing a frame, you'll know not just "does it look good" but "is it actually right for me."

---

## The Main Frame Materials, Grouped into Three Categories

The range of frame materials on the market can look overwhelming, but they really break down into three groups:

- **Plastics:** cellulose acetate, TR90
- **Metals:** stainless steel, monel alloy, pure titanium / beta titanium
- **Composites:** plastic and metal combined, taking advantage of both

Each has situations where it shines. There's no absolute "best" — only "the best fit for your face, your prescription, and how you use your glasses."

## Materials in Detail

### Cellulose Acetate

Acetate is the mainstream material for mid-to-high-end plastic frames today, made from cellulose derived from cotton or wood pulp — a plant-based material.

- **Strengths:** the strongest expressive range of colors and patterns (transparent, tortoiseshell, gradient finishes all rely on it), a warm, substantial feel, skin-friendly, and **almost no metal content, so allergy risk is extremely low.**
- **Things to watch for:** it has some inherent weight, so large or thick frames can be felt on the nose after long wear; prolonged sun or heat exposure can cause slight warping or fading.
- **Adjustability:** acetate can be heated and softened with a heat gun to reshape it — this is exactly what makes hand adjustment so effective at improving comfort, letting the nose pads, face-form curve, and temple arms all be fine-tuned to your face.

Handcrafted acetate from Sabae, Japan, takes the polishing and layered-color finishing to another level entirely — this is where the price gap between good acetate frames and cheap plastic ones comes from.

### Metal Frames: Stainless Steel and Monel

Metal frames are known for being sleek, thin, and clean-lined — the go-to choice for thin-frame lovers.

- **Stainless steel:** high strength, corrosion-resistant, good elasticity, relatively affordable — a common material for entry-level metal frames.
- **Monel:** a nickel-copper alloy, highly malleable, easy to shape and solder — the classic material for traditional metal frames.
- **Things to watch for:** both of these materials **typically contain nickel**. Nickel is one of the most common metal allergens, so if you have sensitive skin, sweat easily, or have ever felt itchy wearing metal jewelry, take note. Good-quality metal frames apply a surface plating to reduce direct skin contact, but if you already know you're allergic to nickel, we'd suggest going straight to pure titanium or acetate instead.

### Pure Titanium / Beta Titanium

Titanium is the top-tier material for high-end frames, and the one we most often recommend for long wear times and high prescriptions.

- **Pure titanium:** lightweight, high strength, **corrosion- and sweat-resistant, and highly biocompatible** — titanium is a mainstream material for medical implants, meaning it's very friendly to body tissue and carries an extremely low risk of allergic reaction.
- **Beta titanium:** a titanium alloy that keeps titanium's light weight and low allergy risk while offering better flexibility and shapeability — often used in temple arms or anywhere that needs spring-back.
- **Why it's especially good for high prescriptions:** high-myopia lenses are already heavy on their own, and a heavy frame on top of that means the whole thing presses uncomfortably on your nose all day. Pure titanium's light weight noticeably reduces that burden, and its sweat resistance and corrosion resistance make it especially friendly for people who sweat easily.

To learn more about pure titanium craftsmanship, see our feature on the Japanese Sabae brand MINAMOTO — its 99.4%-pure titanium, screwless construction is a standout example of pure titanium frame craft.

### TR90

TR90 is a memory plastic (polyamide) that's become widely used in sport frames, kids' frames, and lightweight frames in recent years.

- **Strengths:** **extremely light, highly elastic, and hard to break** — it springs back after bending and resists impact. Metal-free, so allergy risk is low. Budget-friendly.
- **Best for:** active kids, people with sports needs, and anyone who wants the lightest possible frame. TR90 is a popular choice for children's frames precisely because it's drop-resistant and light.
- **Things to watch for:** because it's designed to spring back, **fine-tuned angle adjustments have less range than acetate or metal** — the physical properties of a memory material mean that past a certain point, it just bounces back. We'll flag this upfront if you're considering an adjustment.

### Composite Materials

Composite frames mix different materials — usually a "metal front + acetate temples" or "titanium body + acetate nose pads" — to capture the strengths of both.

- **Common combinations:** the light, clean lines of metal paired with the skin-friendly color and pattern of acetate, or titanium's light weight paired with acetate's warm feel.
- **Things to watch for:** mixed materials need different care and adjustment approaches for different parts, and any metal component still carries the same nickel-allergy considerations.

## Material Doesn't Just Affect Feel — It Affects "How Well It Can Be Adjusted"

This is something many people overlook when choosing a frame: **material directly determines how closely a frame can be adjusted to fit your face.**

- Acetate needs to be heated and softened before it can be shaped, relying on the feel of a heat gun and specialized pliers.
- Pure titanium and beta titanium are strong and highly elastic, requiring the right optical pliers and precise force — forcing them can not only fail to get the fit right, but cause metal fatigue.
- TR90's memory material has its physical limits — past certain angles, it simply springs back.

And how well a frame is adjusted directly affects whether the lens's optical center aligns with your pupil, whether the pantoscopic tilt is correct, and whether the vertex distance is accurate — these are the real answers to "is it comfortable, is it clear." So choosing a material is, in a sense, also choosing how well this pair of glasses can be cared for down the line.

## FAQ

**Q: Is a titanium frame worth the extra money?**

It depends on how you use your glasses. If you wear them more than 8 hours a day, have a higher prescription (meaning heavier lenses), sweat easily, or have had allergic reactions to metal frames before, pure titanium's light weight, sweat resistance, and low allergy risk will make a noticeable difference — and it's usually money well spent. On the other hand, if you only wear glasses occasionally, have a low prescription, or have a limited budget, stainless steel or acetate frames can be plenty comfortable, and you don't necessarily need to go straight for titanium. We'd suggest trying both a titanium frame and a regular frame on in store — you'll feel the weight difference the moment you pick them up.

**Q: Do acetate frames cause allergic reactions?**

Cellulose acetate is a plant-based cellulose material with almost no metal content, making it one of the lowest-allergy-risk materials available — usually a safe choice for people prone to common nickel allergies. A small number of people may have a mild reaction to specific surface coatings or their own individual skin sensitivity, but acetate's skin-friendliness is clearly better than nickel-containing metal frames. If you've ever felt itchy wearing a metal frame, acetate or pure titanium are both worth considering first. (Note: this is general material information — if you have a severe or persistent skin reaction, please consult a dermatologist.)

**Q: Which frame material is the lightest?**

Among mainstream materials, TR90 and pure titanium are the two lightest categories. TR90 is a memory plastic with low density, so frames can be made very light — it's commonly used in kids' frames and sport frames. Pure titanium is one of the lightest metal options while still offering high strength, making it ideal for anyone who wants "light and durable" for long wear. Cellulose acetate is relatively heavier, especially in large or thick frames. Actual wearing weight also depends on frame size and lens thickness/prescription, so it's worth evaluating together with your lenses.
