## A Kid's Glasses Shouldn't Just Be "the Adult Version, but Smaller"

A lot of parents bringing their kids in for glasses instinctively reach for whatever frame looks nice and is cheap, figuring the child will outgrow it or break it anyway, so why not just get something to hold them over.

That thinking is understandable, but it's pointed in the wrong direction.

A child's frame has to handle a completely different set of conditions than an adult's: running, jumping, getting knocked around, tossed wherever, all while their face is still growing. More importantly, if your child is wearing [defocus-design lenses](/en/guide/) for myopia tracking, choosing the wrong frame directly undermines the optical performance those lenses are designed to deliver.

When choosing a kids' frame, there are really only three things that matter: **safety, durability, and fit.**

---

## 1. Material Safety: Soft, Resilient, No Sharp Edges

What parents worry about most is a fall or a bump turning the frame itself into something that hurts the child's face or eyes. So the first principle for kids' frames is that the material itself needs to be safe.

**TR90 and silicone-based materials are the mainstream choice for children's frames:**

- **TR90 (memory plastic):** Lightweight and highly resilient — it bends and springs back rather than snapping, and even under pressure it's less likely to break into a sharp edge.
- **Silicone nose pads and soft temple tips:** Sit gently against the skin without chafing, and reduce slipping.
- **No sharp-edge design:** The frame edges, hinges, and screws should all be rounded off to avoid cuts on impact.

That's not to say metal frames are off-limits for kids — it depends on age and how the child uses their glasses day to day. For younger, more active children, a softer, more resilient material like TR90 is generally the safer bet.

---

## 2. Durability: The Hinges and Structure Are What Really Matter

"Durable" isn't about how hard the frame is — it's about whether it bounces back after being put through the wringer.

Kids take their glasses on and off with one hand, toss them on the desk, and stuff them into their backpacks. The part that usually fails first is the **hinge** — the joint connecting the temple to the frame.

- **Spring hinges:** Let the temple flex outward beyond its normal range and spring back, so when a child pulls or yanks with one hand, the hinge doesn't immediately snap or loosen. This is well worth prioritizing for kids' frames.
- **One-piece molded frames:** Fewer joints means fewer points of failure.
- **Lens mounting method:** Full-rim frames hold up better than half-rim or rimless designs, and the lenses are less likely to pop out.

A more durable frame might look a bit pricier upfront, but with fewer repair trips and fewer broken pairs, it usually ends up cheaper in the long run.

---

## 3. Room to Grow: Adjustability Matters More Than Buying Big

The question we hear most from parents: "Can I just buy a size up, so it still fits once they grow?"

**The answer is no.** We'll get into why in more detail in the FAQ below, but the principle is this: a child's face is still growing, and the right approach isn't to buy an oversized frame and wait for them to grow into it — it's to choose a frame that **fits now, with room left to adjust.**

That adjustment room comes from two design features:

- **Adjustable nose pads:** As the bridge of the nose develops, the height and width of the nose pads can be fine-tuned to keep the glasses sitting at the right height.
- **Temples that can be reshaped and re-bent:** As ear position and head size change, the temples can be reshaped to fit again.

Fit can always be adjusted later; buying the wrong overall size cannot.

---

## 4. Fit: This Step Directly Affects How Well Defocus Lenses Perform

This is the step most often overlooked, yet it's the most technical one. Whether a frame fits isn't just about comfort — it directly affects the lens's optical performance.

**What happens if the frame is too big?**

A lens's **optical center** needs to line up with the child's pupil. If the frame is too large, the pupil ends up offset from the optical center, producing an unintended prismatic effect — the child may find things look slightly "off," get tired more easily, or even feel dizzy.

For children wearing [defocus-design lenses](/en/guide/), this matters even more. This type of lens's optical design depends on specific zones of the lens landing precisely in front of the child's eye. If the frame is off and the optical center shifts, the lens's defocus zones no longer line up with the line of sight, and **the optical design simply can't do its job.**

In other words: paying for defocus-design lenses and then pairing them with an ill-fitting frame means giving up half the optical design you paid for. The details of how frames and lenses should be matched are worth a parent's time to understand.

## 5. Stability: Glasses That Slide Off Are the Same as Not Wearing Them

Active kids end up with their glasses constantly sliding down — which isn't just annoying, it also pushes their line of sight toward the edge of the lens, making things blurry, and the child ends up peering over the top rim of the frame instead. This is especially bad for a child wearing defocus-design lenses.

Stability comes down to a few things:

- **Anti-slip nose pads and temples:** Silicone materials add friction, so the glasses stay put even through sweat and running around.
- **Sports straps (back straps):** Clipped on for gym class or sports, so the glasses don't go flying.
- **Light weight:** The lighter the frame, the less it's pulled down by gravity, and the less strain on the child's nose bridge.
- **Precise adjustment:** Even the best frame needs to be adjusted in person to actually fit this particular child's face — that's what makes it stable.

## FAQ

**Q: What material is best for kids' frames?**

For active children, TR90-type memory plastic is generally the safest choice: lightweight, highly resilient, and springs back after bending, so it's unlikely to snap into a sharp edge even under pressure — paired with silicone nose pads for a better, non-chafing fit. Metal frames aren't off the table, but they suit older children who tend to be more careful with their glasses. The right choice ultimately depends on the child's age, activity level, and face shape, best assessed in person.

**Q: Can I just buy kids' frames a size bigger so they last as they grow?**

We don't recommend it. An oversized frame shifts the child's pupil away from the lens's optical center, which can cause visual instability, eye strain, or even dizziness — and for a child wearing defocus-design lenses, it undercuts the lens's optical design. The right approach is to choose a frame that fits now while leaving room to adjust — using adjustable nose pads and reshapeable temples to fine-tune the fit as your child grows, rather than relying on an oversized frame to "grow into."

**Q: My child's glasses keep sliding off — what should I do?**

Don't rush to replace the glasses — most slipping issues can be fixed with an adjustment. The usual cause is nose pads or temples that aren't adjusted to fit the child's face, and bringing them in for a re-fit usually solves it on the spot. If your child is especially active, silicone anti-slip nose pads or a sports strap for active play can help. Glasses that keep sliding aren't just annoying — they also push the child's line of sight toward the edge of the lens, undermining the fitting, so it's worth addressing sooner rather than later.
