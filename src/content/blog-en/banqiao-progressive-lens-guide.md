## The Day Your Arm Isn't Long Enough Anymore, It's Coming

Somewhere around age 45, most people hit the same moment: you can't quite make out a text message, and without even thinking about it, you hold the phone a little farther away — and suddenly it's readable again.

Your eyes aren't broken. This is **presbyopia** — the lens inside your eye gradually loses its flexibility with age, and its ability to focus up close declines. It's a physiological process everyone goes through eventually, and it has nothing to do with whether you're nearsighted.

The real question was never "do I have presbyopia" — it's **how you're going to work with it.** In Banqiao, there's no shortage of places to get glasses, but whether progressive lenses are actually the right answer for you is worth five minutes of thought before you commit.

## Thing One: Progressive Lenses Aren't the Only Solution for Presbyopia

| Option | Best For | Limitation |
|------|---------|------|
| Single-vision reading glasses | Worn only for reading, simple visual needs | Have to take them off to see distance — on and off all day |
| Office/occupational lenses | Mostly indoors, focused on computer + paperwork | Not suitable for distance (like driving) |
| Progressive multifocal lenses | Need distance, intermediate, and near all in one pair | Requires precise fitting and an adaptation period |
| Bifocal lenses | Budget-conscious, comfortable with a visible dividing line | Visible line on the lens; gap in intermediate vision |

**People whose work is mostly close-range aren't necessarily good candidates for progressives** — this is something many Banqiao customers hear for the first time only after they've come into the store. The right option should be recommended by a licensed optometrist based on your lifestyle, not just whatever's most expensive. ## Thing Two: When Progressives Make You Dizzy, It's Almost Never the Lens's Fault

"I heard progressives make you dizzy" is the biggest mental hurdle for people facing presbyopia. But dizziness is usually caused by one of these:

- **Pantoscopic tilt set incorrectly** — a few degrees off in the angle between the lens and your line of sight, and the peripheral distortion gets amplified
- **Errors in pupillary distance or fitting-height measurement** — the progressive corridor is positioned to the millimeter, and if it's off, nothing looks right no matter where you look
- **Frame drift** — a frame that shifts or loosens over time, taking the optical center with it
- **An incomplete adaptation period** — your brain needs 2–4 weeks to learn the new visual zones

In other words: **whether progressives work out for you is half about the lens, half about the fitting and adjustment.** This is exactly why it matters to choose a store with a thorough eye-exam process and a willingness to provide free follow-up adjustments after the fitting.

## Thing Three: What a 45–60 Minute Exam Gets You That a 10-Minute One Doesn't

A progressive lens prescription isn't just "a number." A full process includes intake, objective and subjective refraction, binocular vision assessment, addition-power (ADD) assessment, and measuring fitting height and pantoscopic tilt — Beyond Visual Optometry's full exam takes about 45–60 minutes, performed one-on-one by a licensed optometrist.

A pair of progressives fitted from a 10-minute computerized refraction has a much higher chance of causing dizziness — that's not a scare tactic, it's just optics.
