## Choosing a Frame Isn't About Trends — It's About Your Face

Most people pick a frame because "this one looks good." But put the same frame on a different face, and the effect can be night and day.

The reason is simple: **a frame is worn on your face, not displayed on a shelf.** Whether a frame looks good was never really about the frame itself — it's about whether it works with your face shape, your features, and your face width.

Face shape is just a starting point, not the only answer — but once you understand it, you have a baseline to judge from, instead of relying on guesswork every time you try something on.

---

## Core Principle 1: Contrast, Not Imitation

This is the most important principle — and the one most often gotten backwards.

**A frame's lines should work against your face shape, not match it.** The goal is to use the frame to balance your face shape so your features look more harmonious.

- **Round faces** (soft lines, full cheeks) → choose frames with angles and defined edges (square, aviator, or polygon frames) — straight lines soften the roundness.
- **Square faces** (defined jawline, squarer forehead and chin) → choose rounded, curved frames (round, oval, or cat-eye frames) — curves soften the angles.

If a round face wears an oversized round frame, the face looks even rounder. If a square face wears a heavy, hard-edged square frame, the lines get even harder. That's the trap of "imitation."

---

## Core Principle 2: Frame Size Should Match Your Features' Proportions

Bigger isn't more fashionable, and smaller isn't more refined. What matters is **whether the frame matches the proportion of your features.**

- If your features are close-set and your face is on the smaller side, an oversized frame can "swallow" your face — all people see is the glasses.
- If your features are prominent and your face is larger, a frame that's too small will look out of proportion and make your face look bigger by contrast.

A simple rule of thumb: **the visual weight of the frame should match the weight of your features.** This is hard to judge accurately in a mirror by yourself — a second pair of eyes watching you try it on in person is far more reliable.

---

## Core Principle 3: Frame Width Should Align with Face Width

Looking straight on, **the outer edge of the frame should roughly line up with the widest point of your cheeks.**

- Frame narrower than your face → your face looks wider by contrast, and the temples can pinch marks into the sides of your head.
- Frame noticeably wider than your face → your features look squeezed toward the center, throwing off the proportions.

The ideal is for the outer edge of the frame to flow naturally into the contour of your face, so your eye travels smoothly from face to frame and back.

---

## Core Principle 4: The Relationship Between Your Eyebrows and the Top of the Frame

This is a more advanced principle, but the effect is immediately noticeable.

**The top edge of the frame should follow or sit close to your eyebrow line.** Eyebrows play a big role in facial recognition, and when the top of the frame echoes your brow shape, your whole face looks more harmonious.

- If the top edge cuts through your eyebrows (the frame sits in the middle of the brow) → it looks like you have two sets of eyebrows, which is jarring.
- If your eyebrows sit far above the frame → your expression can look empty.

Ideally, your eyebrows sit right around the top edge of the frame — just barely visible is the most natural look.

## Face Shape Chart: Recommended Frames vs. Frames to Avoid

| Face Shape | Characteristics | Recommended Frames | Frames to Avoid |
|------|------|----------|----------|
| **Round** | Full cheeks, rounded chin, similar length and width | Square, aviator, or polygon frames (angled, slightly sharp edges) | Oversized round frames, rimless round lenses (reinforce roundness) |
| **Square** | Defined jawline, square forehead and chin | Round, oval, or cat-eye frames (soften the lines) | Heavy, hard-edged square frames (amplify the angles) |
| **Long** | Longer face, greater distance from forehead to chin | Frames with depth and thickness (visually widen the face), dark-colored frames | Narrow, slim frames (elongate the face further) |
| **Heart-shaped** | Wide forehead, narrow chin | Frames with a rounder bottom edge, thin frames, rimless, or cat-eye (balances wide top with narrow bottom) | Top-heavy, chunky frames or aviators (make the forehead look wider) |
| **Oval** | Balanced proportions, widely considered the most versatile | Can carry almost any frame — free to choose based on style | Frames that are too large or too small and thrown off-balance |

> **The oval face is the "goes-with-everything" shape everyone envies** — but that doesn't mean any frame will look good on it. A well-proportioned face can actually handle more variety, so the real question becomes: what style are you going for?

## An Optometrist's Reminder: Looking Good Isn't Enough — the Frame Also Has to Fit Your Lenses

Everything above is about aesthetics. But as an optometry practice, we have to flag something many people don't realize, even though it directly affects your final result:

**The frame you choose determines how good your lenses can actually be.**

- **High myopia:** the higher your prescription, the thicker the lens edge. **The bigger the frame, the thicker and more visible the lens becomes.** People with high prescriptions are actually better off with a smaller, rounder frame with a smaller lens area, to keep thickness and weight down. We cover this in full in our guide to fitting glasses for high myopia.
- **Progressive multifocal lenses:** progressive power changes gradually from top to bottom, so the frame needs **enough vertical height** to fit the distance, intermediate, and near zones. A shallow, narrow frame compresses the near and intermediate viewing areas, making the lenses harder to use in practice. If you're considering progressives, pay close attention to frame height when choosing — see our guide to progressive multifocal lenses for the full picture.

In other words, **choosing a frame means weighing aesthetics and optical needs together.** Focus only on looks, and you might sacrifice visual quality; focus only on optics, and you might end up with a frame you don't actually want to wear. Balancing the two is exactly where professional in-person fitting earns its keep.

## FAQ

**Q: What frames suit a round face?**

Round faces do well with frames that have angles and defined edges — square, aviator, or polygon shapes. The straight lines balance out the roundness of the cheeks, giving the face more definition and structure. Avoid oversized round frames and rimless round lenses, which reinforce the roundness and can make the face look even rounder.

**Q: Should I pick a frame that matches my face shape or contrasts with it?**

As a rule, contrast. A frame's lines work against your face shape to create a balancing effect: angled frames for round faces, rounded frames for square faces. Choosing a frame that matches your face shape (round frames for a round face, square frames for a square face) actually amplifies that shape's features. This is the core "contrast principle" of frame selection.

**Q: What's the single most important thing when choosing a frame?**

If you can only remember one thing, make it "proportion" — frame size should match your features, and frame width should align with your face width. Face shape determines the direction of the frame's lines; proportion determines its size and visual weight. Get both right, and you're most of the way there. But don't forget: if you have a high prescription or are considering progressive lenses, the frame also needs to meet your lenses' optical requirements — and that's best evaluated together with an optometrist in person.
