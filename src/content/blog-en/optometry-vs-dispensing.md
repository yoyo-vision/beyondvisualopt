## "Getting Glasses" and "Dispensing" Aren't the Same Thing

Most people think of "going to get glasses" as one single step: sit down, read the eye chart, pick a frame, go home.

It's not that simple. Hidden inside that process are two distinct professional stages: the **vision exam (optometry)** and **dispensing**. They serve different purposes and produce different results, but both directly determine how comfortable your glasses ultimately feel.

(One clarification up front: this article is about "vision exam vs. dispensing" as **two stages in the process of getting a pair of glasses**—not "optometry practice vs. ophthalmology," which is a different kind of professional division around who handles eye disease versus who handles refraction and dispensing. That's a separate topic we'll unpack again in the FAQ below.)

---

## The Vision Exam: Finding Out What Prescription Your Eyes Need

The vision exam (refraction/optometry) is the **measurement and assessment** stage: confirming your refractive status (the degree of myopia, hyperopia, and astigmatism), your near-vision add power, and your binocular vision function, one item at a time.

This stage deals with "data about you and your eyes." Using equipment and a structured process, it produces a set of values and parameters suited to you—commonly known as your "prescription."

In Taiwan, performing vision exams is the legally defined scope of practice for **licensed optometrists**, and is carried out by them.

---

## Dispensing: Turning the Prescription into a Physical Pair of Glasses

Dispensing comes after the vision exam: once you have your prescription, it covers picking a frame, choosing a lens design, measuring your face and the frame's various parameters, and then fitting, aligning, and gradually adjusting the lenses until they fit properly.

This stage deals with "how to precisely put your prescription into the physical glasses on your face." With the exact same prescription, if the dispensing parameters aren't right, the glasses can feel very different to wear.

Dispensing is just as much a professional skill. Fitting and calibrating a pair of glasses is also carried out by **licensed optometrists**.

## Why Can Glasses Still Feel Wrong Even When "the Prescription Is Right"?

This is where most people get tripped up. **No matter how accurate the vision exam is, if the dispensing parameters aren't set correctly, the glasses will still feel off.** A few common scenarios:

- **Pupillary distance (PD) misalignment**: if the lens's optical center isn't aligned with your pupil, your vision can feel subtly "pulled" or strained.
- **Off-center optical positioning**: high prescriptions and progressive multifocal lenses are especially sensitive to this—if the center is off, the clear zone shifts entirely out of place.
- **Vertex distance and pantoscopic tilt left unadjusted**: how far the lens sits from your eye, and how many degrees the frame tilts forward, both change the effective prescription your eye actually experiences.

So "an accurate vision exam" and "accurate dispensing" are two separate hurdles, and you need to clear both. A pair of glasses that truly fits is the result of getting both stages right.

## Common Misconceptions / FAQ

**Q: Are the vision exam and dispensing done by the same person?**

In Taiwan, both the vision exam and the dispensing/fitting are within a licensed optometrist's professional scope. In practice, one optometrist might handle the whole process, or it might be divided between team members—but either way, both stages should be carried out by a qualified licensed optometrist.

**Q: Can I just get a vision exam and take my prescription elsewhere?**

Yes. A vision exam is a standalone professional service in its own right, and it produces your prescription and related parameters. Whether you get your glasses made at the same location afterward is entirely your choice—just keep in mind that wherever you have them dispensed, that step still requires a complete set of facial and frame measurements for the prescription to be implemented correctly.

**Q: If I just enter my prescription numbers online, does that count as "dispensing"?**

Entering your prescription only covers the "numbers" part. Genuine dispensing also includes pupillary distance, optical center alignment, vertex distance, pantoscopic tilt, and a whole series of personalized measurements and fitting adjustments—things that are very hard to get right without being measured in person. That's exactly why the same prescription can result in very different dispensing quality depending on where it's done.

**Q: How is "vision exam vs. dispensing" different from "optometry practice vs. ophthalmology"?**

They're different. "Vision exam vs. dispensing" refers to the two stages involved in getting a pair of glasses (measuring the prescription vs. making the glasses). "Optometry practice vs. ophthalmology" refers to a professional division of labor—ophthalmologists diagnose and treat eye disease, while licensed optometrists handle refractive exams and dispensing. These are two different-level concepts, so don't mix them up.
