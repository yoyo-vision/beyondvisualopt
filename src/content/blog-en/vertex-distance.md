## Which Distance Is "Vertex Distance," Exactly?

**Vertex distance refers to the horizontal distance between the back surface of the lens and the front surface of the cornea.** In plain terms, it's that small gap between the lens and your eyeball. Standard eye exams typically set the reference vertex distance at **around 12 millimeters (mm)**.

That sounds like a trivial fraction of a centimeter—hardly worth worrying about. But in optics, this small distance is exactly what shapes the prescription your eye "actually experiences," and the higher your prescription, the more pronounced the effect becomes.

## Why Does Distance Change the Effective Power? (In Plain Terms)

Picture using a magnifying glass to focus sunlight into a single point. Move the magnifying glass closer to or farther from the table, and the size and position of that focal point shift along with it. **How strongly a lens bends light is tied to how far it sits from its target (your eye).**

Eyeglass lenses sit in front of your nose, some distance from your eyeball; contact lenses sit directly on the cornea, at essentially zero distance. For the exact same person and the exact same eyes, lenses positioned at different distances need **different powers** to achieve the same focusing effect.

This is exactly how many people discover that their contact lens prescription doesn't match their glasses prescription. It's not that anyone measured wrong—it's that the vertex distance is different, so a conversion is genuinely needed.

## The Higher the Prescription, the Bigger the Difference—±4.00D Is a Common Threshold

At low prescriptions, the difference caused by this distance is small enough to ignore. But once the prescription climbs, the gap gets amplified. The industry generally uses **±4.00D (around 400 degrees)** as the reference point where "vertex distance starts to genuinely matter."

Here's an example using myopic (negative-power) lenses, showing how the power changes when converting from the standard 12mm position to sitting directly on the cornea (the contact lens position):

| Glasses Prescription (Standard 12mm) | Converted to Corneal Position | Difference | Worth Worrying About? |
|---|---|---|---|
| -2.00D (200 degrees) | About -1.95D | Negligible | Can be ignored |
| -4.00D (400 degrees) | About -3.81D | Starting to be noticeable | Worth paying attention to |
| -8.00D (800 degrees) | About -7.30D | Clearly noticeable | Must be calculated |
| -10.00D (1000 degrees) | About -8.93D | Difference over 100 degrees | Must be calculated |

> The figures above are illustrative optical conversions; actual values depend on each individual's specific measurements and refraction data.

Notice that last row: for someone with 1000 degrees of myopia, the difference between wearing a lens in front of the eye versus directly on the cornea works out to **over 100 degrees**. That's well beyond "close enough."

## What This Means in Practice for Fitting

Here's the most tangible takeaway: **for high prescriptions, exactly where the glasses sit on your face really matters.** If the frame slips down a little or gets pushed forward a little, the effective power your eye experiences shifts along with it. That's why people with high prescriptions often complain that "these new glasses look a bit off somehow"—sometimes the issue isn't the prescription itself, but the current position of the frame not matching the vertex distance that was measured during the exam.

Also, **contact lens power can't simply be copied from your glasses prescription.** For people with high prescriptions, using the glasses prescription directly for contacts often misses the mark. This conversion is a matter of professional judgment handled by a licensed optometrist, based on your specific measurements.

Ultimately, this is also part of a proper, personalized fit. Vertex distance is measured by a licensed optometrist, and it gets factored in once you've chosen a frame and confirmed exactly where it sits on your face. The same prescription can land differently depending on the frame and face it's paired with.

## Common Misconceptions / FAQ

**Q: If the standard vertex distance is 12mm, can everyone just use 12mm?**

12mm is a commonly used reference standard, not a hard rule. Everyone's face shape, nose bridge height, and frame design differ, so how far the lens actually sits from the eye varies from person to person. At low prescriptions, using the standard value has little impact; at high prescriptions, a licensed optometrist will measure and calculate based on the actual fitted position rather than applying a fixed number.

**Q: Why is my contact lens prescription lower than my glasses prescription (for myopia)?**

Because contact lenses sit directly on the cornea, with vertex distance essentially at zero, while glasses sit about 12mm from the eye. For myopia (negative power), moving the lens closer to the eye requires slightly less power, and the higher the prescription, the greater that reduction. This is a normal optical conversion, not a measurement error on either side.

**Q: Do people with low prescriptions need to worry about vertex distance?**

Generally, not much. Within ±4.00D, the difference caused by vertex distance is usually small enough to ignore. It mainly becomes a factor that gets amplified at high prescriptions. That said, how securely and correctly a frame sits does affect comfort at any prescription level.

**Q: Do I need to measure vertex distance myself?**

No, and it's not recommended that you do. It's one of the measurements taken during the eye exam process, handled by a licensed optometrist alongside your frame selection. What you should do is wear the frame in its usual position so the measurement reflects how you actually wear your glasses.
